13. Algorithms I: Unconstrained Minimization

Date: 2025-12-16 · Duration: 90 min · Tags: algorithms, unconstrained

Overview: Gradient descent, Newton's method, step size selection, convergence rates, self-concordance.

Prerequisites: Lecture 05

Learning Objectives

After this lecture, you should understand:

  • The convergence properties of gradient descent for strongly convex and merely convex functions.
  • How to implement backtracking line search and why it is preferred over exact search.
  • The derivation and quadratic convergence of Newton's method.
  • The concept of self-concordance and its role in Newton's method analysis.

Key Concepts

0. Notation and the "Model"

The book implicitly works with a function $$ f: \mathrm{dom}\,f \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R} $$ where:

  • $f$ is convex on $\mathrm{dom}\,f$,
  • $f$ is twice continuously differentiable ($f \in C^2$) on $\mathrm{dom}\,f$,
  • $\mathrm{dom}\,f$ is open,
  • The problem is solvable: there exists at least one minimizer $x^\star \in \mathrm{dom}\,f$.

Subtlety: Strictly, if $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$, then $\mathrm{dom}\,f = \mathbb{R}^n$ is open. In convex analysis, we often consider extended-valued functions where $\mathrm{dom}\,f = \{x : f(x) < \infty\}$. Twice differentiability then forces the domain to be open. We write the optimal value as $p^\star = \min_{x \in \mathrm{dom}\,f} f(x) = f(x^\star)$.

1. The Core Equivalence (Section 9.1)

1.1 The Unconstrained Problem

$$ \min_x f(x) \tag{9.1} $$ "Unconstrained" implies no explicit constraints, but we are implicitly constrained to $x \in \mathrm{dom}\,f$. This is crucial for barrier functions (e.g., log barrier), where stepping outside the domain is forbidden.

1.2 Optimality Condition

The necessary and sufficient condition for $x^\star$ to be a global minimizer is: $$ \nabla f(x^\star) = 0 \tag{9.2} $$ For convex functions, "stationary point" upgrades to "global minimizer". This relies on the first-order characterization of convexity: $$ f(y) \ge f(x) + \nabla f(x)^T (y-x) \quad (\star) $$ Proof:

  • Sufficiency: If $\nabla f(x^\star) = 0$, $(\star)$ gives $f(y) \ge f(x^\star)$ for all $y$.
  • Necessity: If $x^\star$ is a minimizer, for any direction $d$, $\phi(t) = f(x^\star + td)$ has a minimum at $t=0$, so $\phi'(0) = \nabla f(x^\star)^T d = 0$. Since this holds for all $d$, $\nabla f(x^\star) = 0$.

2. The Initial Sublevel Set $S$

We define: $$ S = \{ x \in \mathrm{dom}\,f \mid f(x) \le f(x^{(0)}) \} \tag{9.3} $$ Why? Descent methods enforce $f(x^{(k+1)}) \le f(x^{(k)})$, so iterates $x^{(k)}$ remain in $S$. Thus, assumptions need only hold on $S$. We assume $S$ is closed. This ensures that continuous functions (like Hessian eigenvalues) attain extrema on $S$ (if bounded).

3. Motivating Examples

  • Quadratic Minimization: $\min_x \frac{1}{2}x^T P x + q^T x + r$ with $P \succeq 0$. Optimality $\nabla f(x)=0$ leads to linear system $Px = -q$. If $P \succ 0$, unique solution $x^\star = -P^{-1}q$.
  • Least Squares: $\min_x \|Ax-b\|_2^2$. Quadratic with $P=2A^T A$. Optimality gives normal equations $A^T A x = A^T b$.
  • Log-Sum-Exp: $f(x) = \log(\sum e^{a_i^T x + b_i})$. Gradient is weighted average of $a_i$. Hessian is weighted covariance $\succeq 0$.
  • Analytic Center (Log Barrier): $f(x) = -\sum \log(b_i - a_i^T x)$. Barrier term forces minimizer away from boundary. $\nabla^2 f(x) \succeq 0$.

4. Strong Convexity and Its Consequences

We assume strong convexity on $S$: there exists $m > 0$ such that $$ \nabla^2 f(x) \succeq mI, \quad \forall x \in S \tag{9.7} $$ This implies a Quadratic Lower Bound: $$ f(y) \ge f(x) + \nabla f(x)^T(y-x) + \frac{m}{2}\|y-x\|_2^2 \tag{9.8} $$ Interpretation: $f$ lies above its tangent plane by at least a quadratic bowl of curvature $m$.

4.1 Gradient Norm as Suboptimality Certificate

Minimizing the RHS of (9.8) w.r.t. $y$ yields: $$ p^\star \ge f(x) - \frac{1}{2m}\|\nabla f(x)\|^2 $$ Rearranging gives a bound on the objective gap: $$ f(x) - p^\star \le \frac{1}{2m}\|\nabla f(x)\|^2 \tag{9.9} $$ This justifies using $\|\nabla f(x)\| \le \varepsilon$ as a stopping criterion.

4.2 Distance to Solution

$$ \|x - x^\star\| \le \frac{2}{m}\|\nabla f(x)\| \tag{9.11} $$ Strong convexity also implies uniqueness of $x^\star$.

5. Smoothness (Upper Hessian Bound)

Since $S$ is closed and bounded (implied by strong convexity), and $f \in C^2$, there exists $M$ such that: $$ \nabla^2 f(x) \preceq MI, \quad \forall x \in S \tag{9.12} $$ This implies a Quadratic Upper Bound (Descent Lemma): $$ f(y) \le f(x) + \nabla f(x)^T(y-x) + \frac{M}{2}\|y-x\|_2^2 \tag{9.13} $$ This guarantees that stepping in the negative gradient direction decreases $f$.

6. Condition Number of Sublevel Sets

Combining bounds: $mI \preceq \nabla^2 f(x) \preceq MI$. The ratio $\kappa = M/m$ is the condition number. Geometrically, $\kappa$ bounds the "eccentricity" of the sublevel sets. Sublevel sets $C_\alpha$ are sandwiched between two balls of radii ratio $\sqrt{M/m}$. High $\kappa$ implies long, skinny valleys, causing zigzagging in gradient descent.

7. Gradient Descent Analysis

Algorithm: $x^{(k+1)} = x^{(k)} - t^{(k)}\nabla f(x^{(k)})$.

7.1 Exact Line Search

Minimizing the quadratic upper bound (9.13) along descent direction gives step $t=1/M$. Substituting back: $$ f(x^+) \le f(x) - \frac{1}{2M}\|\nabla f(x)\|^2 $$ Using the gradient-gap link $\|\nabla f(x)\|^2 \ge 2m(f(x)-p^\star)$, we get: $$ f(x^+) - p^\star \le \left(1 - \frac{m}{M}\right)(f(x) - p^\star) $$ This is linear (geometric) convergence with rate $c = 1 - 1/\kappa$.

7.2 Backtracking (Armijo) Line Search

Condition: $f(x - tg) \le f(x) - \alpha t \|g\|^2$. Backtracking terminates because for small $t$, linear term dominates quadratic error. It preserves linear convergence with a constant $c < 1$ that depends on $\kappa$. Comparison: Exact line search optimizes the step but doesn't fix the geometry. Zigzagging is due to $\kappa$, not the line search choice.

8. Steepest Descent and General Norms

"Steepest" is relative to a norm $\|\cdot\|$.

8.1 Definition

Normalized Steepest Descent Direction (nsd): $$ \Delta x_{\mathrm{nsd}} = \text{argmin}_{\|v\| \le 1} \nabla f(x)^T v $$ Using the dual norm $\|g\|_* = \sup_{\|v\| \le 1} g^T v$, we have: $$ \nabla f(x)^T \Delta x_{\mathrm{nsd}} = -\|\nabla f(x)\|_* $$ The best linear decrease per unit norm step is exactly the dual norm of the gradient.

8.2 Unnormalized Step

We scale the step for algebraic convenience: $\Delta x_{\mathrm{sd}} = \|\nabla f(x)\|_* \Delta x_{\mathrm{nsd}}$. Then $\nabla f(x)^T \Delta x_{\mathrm{sd}} = -\|\nabla f(x)\|_*^2$.

8.3 Special Cases

  • Euclidean ($\ell_2$): Dual is $\ell_2$. $\Delta x_{\mathrm{sd}} = -\nabla f(x)$ (Gradient Descent).
  • Quadratic ($\|v\|_P = \sqrt{v^T P v}$): Dual is $\|g\|_{P^{-1}} = \sqrt{g^T P^{-1} g}$. $$ \Delta x_{\mathrm{sd}} = -P^{-1}\nabla f(x) $$ This is Preconditioned Gradient Descent. Equivalent to GD in transformed coordinates $\bar{x} = P^{1/2}x$. Choosing $P \approx \nabla^2 f(x)$ makes condition number $\approx 1$.
  • $\ell_1$ Norm: Dual is $\ell_\infty$. $\Delta x_{\mathrm{sd}} = -(\partial f/\partial x_k) e_k$ where $k = \text{argmax}_i |\partial f/\partial x_i|$. This is Coordinate Descent (updates one coordinate at a time).

9. Newton's Method

Newton step: $\Delta x_{\mathrm{nt}} = -\nabla^2 f(x)^{-1} \nabla f(x)$.

9.1 Interpretations

  1. Steepest Descent in Hessian Norm: Steepest descent using $\|\cdot\|_{\nabla^2 f(x)}$. We update the norm (geometry) at every step to match local curvature.
  2. Quadratic Model Minimization: Minimizer of the second-order Taylor approximation $\hat{f}(x+v)$.
  3. Affine Invariance: Unlike GD, Newton's method is invariant to linear coordinate changes $x = Ty$.

9.2 Newton Decrement

$$ \lambda(x) = \left( \nabla f(x)^T \nabla^2 f(x)^{-1} \nabla f(x) \right)^{1/2} $$ It measures the "length" of the Newton step in the Hessian norm. Also, $f(x) - \inf_v \hat{f}(x+v) = \lambda(x)^2/2$. It is a rigorous stopping criterion.

9.3 Convergence Analysis (The "Two-Phase" Story)

  • Damped Phase: When far from optimum ($\lambda(x)$ large), we use backtracking. The method behaves like steepest descent with guaranteed constant decrease per step. This phase lasts finitely many steps.
  • Quadratically Convergent Phase: Once sufficiently close (controlled by Lipschitz Hessian or self-concordance), backtracking accepts $t=1$. The error squares at each step: $$ \|\nabla f(x^{(k+1)})\|_2 \le C \|\nabla f(x^{(k)})\|_2^2 $$ Precision doubles at every step. This is extremely fast ($\log \log (1/\varepsilon)$).

10. Self-Concordance

A function is self-concordant if $|f'''(x)| \le 2 f''(x)^{3/2}$. For such functions, Newton's method can be analyzed without unknown constants like $L, \mu$. The analysis guarantees convergence based purely on the Newton decrement $\lambda(x)$. Standard barriers like $-\sum \log x_i$ are self-concordant.

11. Quasi-Newton Methods

Methods like BFGS approximate the Hessian (or its inverse) using gradient differences $y_k = \nabla f(x_{k+1}) - \nabla f(x_k)$ and steps $s_k = x_{k+1} - x_k$, satisfying the secant equation $B_{k+1} s_k = y_k$. They achieve superlinear convergence without $O(n^3)$ Hessian inversion.

Example Problems

Example 13.1: Gradient Descent on Quadratic

Problem: Apply GD to $f(x) = \frac{1}{2}(x_1^2 + \gamma x_2^2)$ with $\gamma = 10$. Start at $(10, 1)$. Estimate steps to reach $\|x\| \le 10^{-3}$.

Solution:

Condition number $\kappa = 10$. Optimal step rate $t = 2/(L+\mu) = 2/(10+1) \approx 0.18$. Convergence factor $\rho = (\kappa-1)/(\kappa+1) = 9/11 \approx 0.818$. Error decays as $(0.818)^k$. Need $(0.818)^k \cdot \sqrt{101} \le 10^{-3}$. $k \log(0.818) \le \log(10^{-4})$. $k \approx 45$. With exact line search, zigzagging occurs.

Example 13.2: Newton's Method for Logistic Regression

Problem: Formulate Newton step for logistic regression.

Solution:

$f(w) = \sum \log(1 + e^{z_i}) - y_i z_i$ where $z_i = w^\top x_i$. Gradient $\nabla f = X^\top (p - y)$ where $p_i = \sigma(z_i)$. Hessian $H = X^\top D X$ where $D_{ii} = p_i(1-p_i)$. Newton step $\Delta w = -H^{-1} g = -(X^\top D X)^{-1} X^\top (p-y)$. This is iteratively reweighted least squares (IRLS).

Example 13.3: Backtracking Line Search

Problem: Describe the backtracking condition for $f(x) = x^2$ with $\Delta x = -1$ at $x=2$.

Solution:

Condition: $f(x+t\Delta x) \le f(x) + \alpha t f'(x)\Delta x$. $f(2)=4, f'(2)=4, \Delta x=-1$. $(2-t)^2 \le 4 + \alpha t (4)(-1) = 4 - 4\alpha t$. $4 - 4t + t^2 \le 4 - 4\alpha t$. $t^2 \le 4t(1-\alpha) \implies t \le 4(1-\alpha)$. For small $t$, this always holds. We start with $t=1$. If $1 > 4(1-\alpha)$, we reduce $t$. E.g., if $\alpha=0.25$, limit is $3$. $t=1$ is accepted immediately.

Example 13.4: Conjugate Gradient Direction

Problem: For $f(x) = \frac{1}{2}x^\top A x - b^\top x$, how is the search direction updated?

Solution:

$d_{k+1} = -g_{k+1} + \beta_k d_k$. For quadratic CG, $\beta_k = \frac{g_{k+1}^\top g_{k+1}}{g_k^\top g_k}$ (Fletcher-Reeves). Ensures $d_{k+1}^\top A d_k = 0$ ($A$-conjugacy). This allows exact minimization in $n$ steps.

Example 13.5: BFGS Update Rank

Problem: Show that the BFGS update is a rank-2 update.

Solution:

Update: $B_{k+1} = B_k + \frac{y y^\top}{y^\top s} - \frac{B s s^\top B}{s^\top B s}$. This adds two rank-1 matrices. It preserves positive definiteness if $y^\top s > 0$ (curvature condition). Avoids $O(n^3)$ cost of inverting Hessian.

Example 13.6: Convergence Rate Comparison

Problem: Compare GD and Newton on $f(x) = x^4$.

Solution:

GD: $x_{k+1} = x_k - t 4 x_k^3$. For convergence, need small $t$. Rate is sublinear (slow). Newton: $\Delta x = -f'/f'' = -4x^3 / 12x^2 = -x/3$. $x_{k+1} = x_k - x_k/3 = (2/3)x_k$. Linear convergence with rate $2/3$. Note: $x^4$ is strictly convex but not strongly convex at 0 ($f''(0)=0$). Newton is not quadratic here!

Example 13.7: Nesterov Acceleration

Problem: Write the update equations for Nesterov's accelerated gradient.

Solution:

$x_k$: current position, $y_k$: momentum variable. 1. $x_{k+1} = y_k - t \nabla f(y_k)$ 2. $y_{k+1} = x_{k+1} + \frac{k-1}{k+2} (x_{k+1} - x_k)$ Step 1 is standard GD step from the lookahead position $y_k$. Step 2 adds momentum. Converges as $1/k^2$ for smooth convex functions.

Example 13.8: Newton Decrement Property

Problem: Show $\lambda(x)^2 = \nabla f(x)^\top \Delta x_{nt}$.

Solution:

$\Delta x_{nt} = -H^{-1} g$. $\lambda(x)^2 = g^\top H^{-1} g$. Inner product: $g^\top \Delta x_{nt} = g^\top (-H^{-1} g) = -g^\top H^{-1} g = -\lambda(x)^2$. So $\lambda^2 = -g^\top \Delta x_{nt}$ (directional derivative in Newton direction is $-\lambda^2$).

Exercises

  1. Gradient Descent Rate:
    Prove that for $f(x) = \frac{1}{2}x^\top Q x$, GD with optimal constant step size converges as $\|x_{k+1}\| \le \frac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}\|x_k\|$.
    Solution: $x_{k+1} = (I - tQ)x_k$. Minimize spectral radius $\rho(I - tQ) = \max_i |1 - t\lambda_i|$. Optimal $t = 2/(\lambda_{\min} + \lambda_{\max})$. $\rho = \frac{\lambda_{\max} - \lambda_{\min}}{\lambda_{\max} + \lambda_{\min}} = \frac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}$.
  2. Affine Invariance of Newton's Method:
    Let $y = Tx$. Define $g(y) = f(Ty) = f(x)$. Show that the Newton step for $g$ at $y$ corresponds to the Newton step for $f$ at $x$ transformed by $T^{-1}$.
    Solution: $\nabla g(y) = T^\top \nabla f(x)$. $\nabla^2 g(y) = T^\top \nabla^2 f(x) T$. $\Delta y = -[T^\top H T]^{-1} T^\top g = -T^{-1} H^{-1} T^{-\top} T^\top g = -T^{-1} H^{-1} g = T^{-1} \Delta x$. So updates are consistent: $y + \Delta y = T(x + \Delta x)$.
  3. Backtracking Termination:
    Prove that backtracking line search terminates if $f$ has Lipschitz gradient.
    Solution: Lipschitz gradient implies $f(y) \le f(x) + \nabla f(x)^\top(y-x) + \frac{L}{2}\|y-x\|^2$. With $y = x - t\nabla f$, $f(y) \le f(x) - t\|\nabla f\|^2 + \frac{Lt^2}{2}\|\nabla f\|^2$. We want $f(y) \le f(x) - \alpha t \|\nabla f\|^2$. Satisfied if $-t + Lt^2/2 \le -\alpha t \implies Lt/2 \le 1-\alpha \implies t \le \frac{2(1-\alpha)}{L}$. Since we reduce $t$ by factor $\beta$, we eventually drop below this threshold.
  4. Conjugate Gradient Finite Termination:
    Explain why CG terminates in $n$ steps for $n$-dimensional quadratic problems.
    Solution: CG generates search directions $d_0, \dots, d_{n-1}$ that are $Q$-conjugate ($d_i^\top Q d_j = 0$). These form a basis for $\mathbb{R}^n$. Minimizing a convex quadratic sequentially along conjugate directions is equivalent to minimizing over their span. After $n$ steps, we minimize over full $\mathbb{R}^n$.
  5. BFGS Positive Definiteness:
    Show that if $B_k \succ 0$ and $y_k^\top s_k > 0$, then $B_{k+1} \succ 0$.
    Solution: $B_{k+1} = B_k + \frac{y y^\top}{y^\top s} - \frac{B s s^\top B}{s^\top B s}$. Evaluate quadratic form $z^\top B_{k+1} z$. Let $a = y^\top s > 0, b = s^\top B s > 0$. Use Cauchy-Schwarz type inequality or determinant formula. Intuitively, we add a rank-1 PSD term and subtract a rank-1 term, but in a way that preserves PD nature if curvature condition holds. Formal proof uses product form of inverse update (DFP) or determinant update.
